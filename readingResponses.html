Week 2

the 'sheep market' experiment explained through Jeff Howe's 'The rise of crowdsourcing' lens

I find the 'sheep market' to be a very humorous, yet staggeringly clear depiction of what 'the rise of crowdsourcing' author, Jeff Howe, is demonstrating in his text. In a lot of ways, namely Amazon's Mechanical Turk, explained in the article and exemplified in the 'sheep market' experiment/piece, crowdsourcing can undeniably come to the expense of expertise and skills; however, this is arguably made by intentional design. By simplifying the process of creation, may it be professional stock photos or physics and chemistry 'challenges', the crowdsourcing model is able to come down to elementary and structured steps that are easier, quicker and much more efficient to complete, even for someone with little to no background in whichever activity they are meant to perform. The process of crowdsourced labor tasks like the ones previously mentioned come down to anthropomorphizing the assembly line; making every steps of a project as easily manageable by virtually anyone.

In the 'sheep market' experiment/piece, the 10,000 workers exemplify this by drawing a "sheep facing left" in as little time as possible in order to collect their 0,02$. For the makers of 'sheep market', crowdsourcing comes down to 'you get what you pay for', for the better or the worse. The experiment does not seem to draw any conclusion or even opinion from the results, rather suggesting it exists merely to expose what it is you get when you want to pay this kind of money. The signal-to-noise being rather small (10,000 satisfying results, for only 662 rejected ones) may seem to encourage such labor models, while the subjective definition of what is in fact qualified as 'satisfying result' may reflect instead what 0,02$ labor is actually worth, and critiquing the crowdsourcing model for lowered quality work.
